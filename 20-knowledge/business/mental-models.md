# Mental Models

> Thinking tools borrowed from multiple disciplines to improve decision-making and problem-solving.

---

## First Principles

Break problems down to fundamental truths, build up from there.

**Example:**
> "Why does a battery cost so much?" → What are batteries made of? → What do those materials cost?

**Use when:** Challenging assumptions, innovation, cost optimization

---

## Inversion

Instead of asking "How do I succeed?", ask "How do I avoid failure?"

**Example:**
- "How do I build a great culture?" → Invert → "What ruins culture?"
- Avoid: politics, unclear expectations, no feedback

**Use when:** Risk mitigation, avoiding mistakes

---

## Second-Order Thinking

Think about consequences of consequences.

**Example:**
> "Cut prices to increase sales" → Competitors cut prices → Price war → Lower margins for everyone

**Use when:** Complex decisions, avoiding unintended consequences

---

## Occam's Razor

The simplest explanation is usually correct.

**Example:**
> Server down → Simple: API key expired vs Complex: DDoS attack

**Use when:** Debugging, investigation, avoiding over-complication

---

## Hanlon's Razor

Never attribute to malice what can be explained by incompetence.

**Example:**
> Colleague missed deadline → Not sabotage, probably poor planning

**Use when:** Managing relationships, giving benefit of doubt

---

## Opportunity Cost

The cost of something is what you give up to get it.

**Example:**
> Spending 6 months on Feature X → Not working on Feature Y that might have 2x impact

**Use when:** Resource allocation, prioritization

---

## Pareto Principle (80/20)

80% of effects come from 20% of causes.

**Applications:**
- 80% of bugs from 20% of code
- 80% of revenue from 20% of customers
- 80% of impact from 20% of effort

**Use when:** Prioritization, focusing effort

---

## Parkinson's Law

Work expands to fill the time available.

**Implications:**
- Set aggressive deadlines
- Time-box tasks
- Ship quickly

---

## Local vs Global Optima

Optimizing locally may hurt globally.

**Example:**
> Team optimizes for sprint velocity → Technical debt accumulates → Global slowdown

**Use when:** System design, avoiding local optimization traps

---

## Network Effects

Value increases as more people use it.

**Types:**
- **Direct** — More users = more value (WhatsApp)
- **Indirect** — More users attract more developers (iOS)
- **Data** — More users = better product (Google)

**Use when:** Evaluating products, building platforms

---

## Lindy Effect

The longer something survives, the longer it's likely to survive.

**Example:**
> Books in print 100 years → Likely in print 100 more
> New startup → High failure probability

**Use when:** Choosing technologies, evaluating stability

---

## Redundancy & Margin of Safety

Systems that can handle failure.

**Examples:**
- Don't hire to 100% capacity
- Keep 6 months runway
- Have backup systems

**Use when:** Risk management, building resilient systems

---

## Feedback Loops

Circular cause-and-effect relationships.

**Reinforcing (R):** Growth → Investment → More growth
**Balancing (B):** Growth → Competition → Less growth

**Use when:** Understanding system dynamics

---

## Regression to the Mean

Extreme outcomes tend toward average over time.

**Example:**
> Startup has breakout quarter → Next quarter likely closer to average

**Use when:** Evaluating performance, avoiding overreaction

---

## Probabilistic Thinking

Think in probabilities, not certainties.

**Example:**
> "Will this feature succeed?" → "60% chance, worth testing"

**Techniques:**
- Expected value calculations
- Sensitivity analysis
- Scenario planning

---

## Causal vs Correlational

Correlation ≠ Causation.

**Example:**
> Ice cream sales correlate with drownings → Both caused by summer heat

**Use when:** Data analysis, avoiding false conclusions

---

## Survivorship Bias

Only seeing successes, missing failures.

**Example:**
> Studying successful startups → Missing all the failed ones

**Use when:** Learning from examples, avoiding survivorship traps

---

## Confirmation Bias

Seeking information that confirms beliefs.

**Counter-measures:**
- Seek disconfirming evidence
- "Red team" your ideas
- Premortems

**Use when:** Making important decisions

---

## Compounding

Small gains compound over time.

**Examples:**
- 1% better daily = 37x in a year
- Marginal cost reductions
- Knowledge accumulation

**Use when:** Long-term decisions, habits

---

## Map vs Territory

Models are simplified representations, not reality.

**Example:**
> Business plan ≠ Actual business
> Metrics ≠ Real customer value

**Use when:** Using models/frameworks, avoiding model over-reliance
